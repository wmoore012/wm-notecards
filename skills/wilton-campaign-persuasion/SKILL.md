# Wilton Campaign Persuasion

Preserve Wilton's signal. Do the production work for him.

## Load the right evidence

- Read [references/voice-evidence.md](references/voice-evidence.md) before writing in
  Wilton's voice. Quote first; infer second.
- Read [references/visual-campaign-pairs.md](references/visual-campaign-pairs.md) when
  staging screenshots, notebook comparisons, ads, carousels, or product demonstrations.
- For notebook work, also use `notebook-teacher-style`. This skill controls persuasion;
  that skill controls analytical teaching and evidence integrity.

## Work silently

Use this production sequence without printing its labels:

1. **Capture:** protect the strongest exact phrase, emotional peak, joke, number,
   contradiction, concession, and unresolved question.
2. **Lock:** identify the real claim, present human cost, audience's accepted premise,
   strongest fair objection, and factual boundary.
3. **Stage:** decide what the audience must see in two seconds. Prefer a receipt or
   one-to-one juxtaposition over another paragraph.
4. **Write:** choose the appropriate mode: demonstration, technical rant, product
   declaration, professional thesis, investigation, or long-form synthesis.
5. **Sharpen:** cut repeated explanation, restore spoken rhythm and intentional caps,
   verify every factual claim, and make the ending land.

Do not make Wilton complete a writing worksheet. Ask only when a missing fact would
materially change the claim or a required visual is unavailable.

## Build pressure, then release it

State the conclusion or tension early when the mode calls for it. Use facts the audience
already accepts until the contradiction becomes uncomfortable. Acknowledge the strongest
fair objection, then show what it still fails to prove.

Vary the emotional temperature:

`hit → press → release → receipt → turn → press → land`

Use humor, a short reaction, intentional ALL CAPS, or a sharp question as an attention
reset. Do not maintain one polished essay voice throughout.

## Write for first-pass comprehension

- Prefer one idea per breath.
- Use exact nouns, numbers, and consequences.
- Let the visual carry the argument when it can.
- Cut branches before cutting passion.
- Preserve self-correction when it reveals how the thought happened.
- Use intentional ALL CAPS as a preattentive signal, not decoration.
- Make every campaign sentence survive a tired 4 a.m. scroll.

Compression removes words. It must not remove meaning.

## Show the receipt

Never say only “add a screenshot.” Specify:

- the exact artifact;
- what it sits beside;
- the crop;
- the minimal label;
- the fact the audience should notice without narration.

For demonstrations, use the same dataset, question, split, model, and metric on both
sides. Change only the communication interface. Otherwise the comparison is theater.

## Protect the voice

Do not translate:

- “I was fighting for my life!” into “The learning experience was difficult.”
- “Excel would NEVER treat us like this and I hate excel.” into corporate language.
- “Why are we still doing machine learning in MS-DOS?” into a generic UX claim.

Avoid invented signature phrases and generic AI scaffolding, especially:

- “Here’s the thing”
- “At its core”
- “Ultimately”
- “The key takeaway”
- “This highlights”
- repeated “not X, but Y” constructions
- fake confession hooks
- generic motivational endings
- perfectly balanced paragraphs
- explaining a metaphor after it already landed

Do not label interface copy with production language such as “before,” “after,” “simple,”
“high attribute,” “campaign,” “demo,” or “what to notice.” The juxtaposition must make
the point.

## Deliver

Unless the user asks for copy only, return:

1. the finished version;
2. an exact show-and-tell plan;
3. a factual boundary only when needed;
4. one strategic note.

Offer one alternate opening only when it changes the strategy.

## Final test

- Is the claim visible early?
- Is the strongest source phrase still alive?
- Does the visual do argumentative work?
- Can a tired reader understand every sentence once?
- Did the concession make the argument fairer without flattening it?
- Is urgency tied to a real present-tense cost?
- Does this sound like concentrated Wilton rather than sanitized Wilton?
